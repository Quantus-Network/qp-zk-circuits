\begin{abstract}
Post-quantum signature schemes impose large public keys and signatures, increasing on-chain costs while still leaking transaction structure. We present \emph{Quantus Wormhole}, a private settlement protocol where users deposit to addresses derived from secrets and later prove in zero knowledge that the deposit exists, hasn't been spent, and satisfies fee rules. Recursive aggregation amortizes verification across many withdrawals, while dummy padding and local shuffling enlarge the anonymity set for balance-linking adversaries. We formalize the withdrawal relation, analyze privacy guarantees, prove security properties, and report benchmarks for two-layer aggregation of up to 256 leaf proofs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The post-quantum transition poses a dilemma for blockchains. Shor's algorithm breaks elliptic-curve signatures \citep{shor}, but post-quantum replacements like ML-DSA impose keys and signatures an order of magnitude larger \citep{mldsa}. A na\"ive swap increases bandwidth and state growth while doing nothing for privacy. Every transaction still reveals sender, recipient, and amount.

One response is to verify post-quantum signatures inside a zero-knowledge circuit and aggregate many such proofs, compressing the on-chain footprint. But this requires implementing post-quantum signature verification in-circuit, which is expensive.

This paper takes a different approach: rather than proving signature validity, users prove knowledge of a secret tied to an unspent deposit. This idea appears in Ziggy \citep{ziggy}, a STARK-based signature scheme where signers prove knowledge of a hash preimage. We apply the same principle to blockchain settlement, building on EIP-7503's ``wormhole'' concept \citep{eip7503}: funds are sent to addresses derived from secrets and later withdrawn by proving knowledge of that secret. This sidesteps post-quantum signature verification entirely. The circuit only needs hash functions and storage-proof verification. We add recursive proof aggregation for scalable settlement.

\paragraph{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item A formal specification of the wormhole-address withdrawal relation, covering nullifier derivation, authenticated state inclusion, and fee constraints.
    \item A two-layer recursive aggregation architecture where the first layer performs privacy-sensitive dummy padding and shuffling on the client, while subsequent layers can be delegated.
    \item Combinatorial analysis of anonymity set size under the first-layer grouping rules.
    \item Security proofs for deposit binding, one-time withdrawal, and value conservation, with explicit separation of proof-level and chain-level assumptions.
    \item Benchmarks demonstrating 20~ms leaf proving, sub-4~ms verification, and two-layer aggregation scaling to 256 leaves.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Design Goals}

\subsection{Wormholes and Privacy Pools}

EIP-7503 proposes \emph{zero-knowledge wormholes}: deposit to secret-derived addresses, then prove knowledge of the secret to withdraw \citep{eip7503}. We extend this concept with post-quantum proving and recursive aggregation with dummy padding for privacy.

Related systems include privacy pools and shielded ledgers like Zcash and Tornado Cash \citep{eip7503,privacypools,zcash,tornadocash}. Unlike fully shielded ledgers, Quantus proves against native chain state without a separate note commitment tree. Importantly, no ciphertexts are stored on-chain, so a future break in cryptographic assumptions cannot retroactively compromise transaction privacy.

\subsection{Transparent Recursion}

We use Plonky2, combining PLONK arithmetization with FRI commitments for recursive verification without trusted setup \citep{plonky2,fri}. FRI is based on hash functions rather than elliptic curves, making it plausibly post-quantum secure—no known quantum algorithm offers more than a quadratic speedup against hash preimage or collision resistance. Poseidon2 provides efficient in-circuit hashing over prime fields \citep{poseidon2}.

\paragraph{Security Parameters:}
Our instantiation uses the Goldilocks field (\(p = 2^{64} - 2^{32} + 1\)) with a quadratic extension for 100-bit soundness. FRI is configured with 28 query rounds, rate \(2^3\), and 16 bits of proof-of-work grinding, yielding \(28 \times 3 + 16 = 100\) bits of security against proof forgery. Poseidon2 outputs 4 field elements (256 bits), providing approximately 128-bit collision resistance. Under Grover's algorithm, hash security degrades to roughly 64 bits against a quantum adversary with sufficient qubits; increasing to a larger field or more rounds would restore the classical margin.

\subsection{Design goals}

The protocol aims to satisfy four simultaneous goals:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Amortized Verification:} The marginal on-chain cost of verifying an additional withdrawal should be small.
    \item \textbf{Deposit Binding:} Every withdrawal must be bound to a real deposit recorded in authenticated chain state.
    \item \textbf{Replay Prevention:} A deposit should not authorize multiple withdrawals; nullifiers must uniquely bind proofs to deposits.
    \item \textbf{Meaningful Privacy:} The chain should reveal only the public information needed for settlement: exits, amounts, shared fee parameters, nullifiers, and a block hash. Funding accounts, secrets, and raw storage proofs should remain private.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System, Adversary, and Trust Model}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Roles}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{User / Prover:} Generates a secret, derives a wormhole address, deposits funds, and later produces a zero-knowledge proof to withdraw. First-layer aggregation should be performed locally to preserve privacy.
    \item \textbf{Aggregator:} Combines multiple proofs into a single aggregate proof. This role may be the user, a third-party service, or a block producer. Higher-layer aggregation can be delegated since it operates only on public outputs.
    \item \textbf{Blockchain / Verifier:} Validates the final proof, checks nullifier freshness, and settles withdrawals to the specified exit accounts.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Adversarial capabilities}

We assume a probabilistic polynomial-time adversary that can:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item observe the full chain and all public proof outputs,
    \item submit arbitrary deposits,
    \item perform timing analysis, and
    \item attempt to replay or malform proof submissions.
\end{itemize}
The adversary cannot:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item break the collision resistance of the hash function,
    \item break the knowledge soundness of the proof system, or
    \item violate the chain's block-history availability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Trust assumptions}

The protocol relies on the following assumptions.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Hash Security:} The Poseidon2 instantiation used by the system remains collision resistant and preimage resistant for the relevant security level \citep{poseidon2}.
    \item \textbf{Proof Soundness:} The configured Plonky2 recursion stack remains knowledge sound under its standard assumptions \citep{plonky2,fri}.
    \item \textbf{Authenticated History:} The blockchain can check that the public block hash and block number correspond to a valid block in chain history.
    \item \textbf{Witness Availability:} Honest users can obtain the storage proof needed to build a witness for their deposit.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Protocol Overview}
\label{sec:overview}

The protocol consists of five stages.

\paragraph{1. Secret Generation and Deposit:}
The user samples a secret \(s\) and derives a wormhole address
\[
\WA(s) = H(H(\mathsf{salt_{wh}} \parallel s)).
\]
Because this address is derived from a hash rather than a public key, no one can spend from it via ordinary signature-based authorization—wormhole addresses can receive funds but never have outgoing transactions. Anyone can deposit to a wormhole address through an ordinary on-chain transfer, creating a record that can later be authenticated inside the circuit. A user may receive funds from their own prior transfers or from third-party payments to wormhole addresses derived from secrets the user controls.

\paragraph{2. Witness Acquisition:}
To withdraw, the user gathers the deposit record and a storage proof. These form the private witness.

\paragraph{3. Leaf Proof Generation:}
The user chooses up to two exit outputs, computes a nullifier
\[
\Null(s,c) = H(H(\mathsf{salt_{null}} \parallel s \parallel c)),
\]
and produces a leaf proof. The proof reveals the nullifier, the exits, the output amounts, the asset identifier, the fee basis points, the block hash, and the block number, while keeping the secret and storage proof private. Leaf proofs are not zero-knowledge. They are only seen by the user who generated them and then included in the first aggregation layer as private inputs.

\paragraph{4. First-Layer Aggregation:}
The user batches leaf proofs that share the same \texttt{block\_hash}, pads with dummy leaves if needed, shuffles the order, and produces a zero-knowledge wrapper proof that forwards grouped exits and nullifiers. A user may combine multiple deposits from distinct wormhole addresses (each derived from a different secret) into a single aggregated withdrawal. This step should be performed locally to preserve privacy.

\paragraph{5. Settlement:}
The blockchain verifies the aggregate proof, checks that the public \texttt{block\_hash} matches the referenced block number, verifies that the fee parameter matches the configured rate, rejects reused nullifiers, and settles the exits.

\section{Formal Specification and Interfaces}
\label{sec:formal}

\subsection{Leaf public inputs}

The leaf proof exposes the following public fields:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \texttt{asset\_id}: Token identifier.
    \item \texttt{output\_amount\_1}, \texttt{output\_amount\_2}: Exit amounts (secondary is zero if unused).
    \item \texttt{fee\_bps}: Fee parameter in basis points.
    \item \texttt{nullifier}: Replay-prevention tag derived from secret and counter.
    \item \texttt{exit\_account\_1}, \texttt{exit\_account\_2}: Destination accounts.
    \item \texttt{block\_hash}: Commitment to block header fields.
    \item \texttt{block\_number}: Height of the referenced block.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Leaf private witness}

The private witness includes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \texttt{secret}: User secret from which nullifier and wormhole address are derived.
    \item \texttt{counter}: Per-deposit counter used in nullifier derivation.
    \item \texttt{wormhole\_account}: Derived wormhole address \(\WA(s)\).
    \item \texttt{storage\_proof}: Inclusion proof for the deposit record.
    \item \texttt{input\_amount}: Deposited amount before fee deduction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Leaf relation}

Let \(x = (a, v_1, v_2, f, n, \alpha_1, \alpha_2, h_B, b)\) denote the public input tuple and let \(w\) denote the witness described above. The leaf relation \(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{leaf}}\) holds when there exists a witness \(w\) such that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(C\arabic*),leftmargin=1.9em]
    \item \textbf{Nullifier Correctness:} \(n = \Null(s,c)\).
    \item \textbf{Wormhole-Address Correctness:} \(\alpha_{\mathrm{wh}} = \WA(s)\).
    \item \textbf{Authenticated Inclusion:} The storage proof verifies that the deposit record exists in chain state.
    \item \textbf{Block Binding:} The storage proof is bound to the public block hash \(h_B\) and block number \(b\).
    \item \textbf{Fee Constraint:} The disclosed exits satisfy
    \[
    (v_1 + v_2) \cdot 10000 \leq v_{\mathrm{in}} \cdot (10000 - f).
    \]
\end{enumerate}

The inequality in (C5) allows users to withdraw less than their full balance. This provides a privacy benefit: by forfeiting some value, a user can choose output amounts that blend with more deposits, enlarging their anonymity set. The difference between the deposit and the sum of outputs (beyond fees) is effectively burned.

\subsection{On-chain verification}

The proof system does not, by itself, settle a withdrawal. The blockchain must also:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item verify the referenced block exists in chain history
    \item check the fee parameter matches the configured rate
    \item reject previously-seen nullifiers
    \item credit exit accounts without increasing total supply
\end{itemize}

\section{Recursive Aggregation Architecture}
\label{sec:aggregation}

\subsection{First-layer aggregation}

The first aggregation layer is privacy-sensitive and should be executed locally. A batch of up to \(N\) leaf proofs is aggregated as follows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item All real leaves must share the same \texttt{block\_hash}, \texttt{asset\_id}, and \texttt{fee\_bps}.
    \item If fewer than \(N\) real leaves are available, the batch is padded with dummy leaves.
    \item The leaf order is shuffled before wrapping.
    \item Exits to identical accounts are grouped into a single summed output.
    \item Real nullifiers are forwarded; dummy nullifiers are replaced by hashing prover-supplied random preimages, preventing a malicious prover from choosing a dummy nullifier that collides with a real one.
\end{itemize}

A delegated first-layer service would observe raw child proofs before padding and shuffling, collapsing much of the anonymity set quantified in \cref{sec:layer0privacy}.

\subsection{Higher-layer aggregation}

Higher layers aggregate already-wrapped proofs. Since they operate only on public outputs, they can be safely delegated and do not require zero-knowledge, enabling faster proving.

\section{Security and Privacy Analysis}
\label{sec:security}

We formalize security via three games capturing the properties a wormhole system must satisfy. Let \(\mathcal{B}\) denote a probabilistic polynomial-time adversary, \(H\) a hash function, and \(\Pi = (\mathsf{Prove}, \mathsf{Verify})\) the proof system with knowledge soundness error \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{ks}}\).

\subsection{Deposit binding}

\begin{definition}[Deposit Binding Game]
The \emph{deposit binding game} \(\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{bind}}^{\mathcal{B}}\) proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) is given access to the blockchain state and may create arbitrary deposits.
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) outputs a proof \(\pi\) and public inputs \(x = (a, v_1, v_2, f, n, \alpha_1, \alpha_2, h_B, b)\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) wins if \(\mathsf{Verify}(\pi, x) = 1\) and no deposit to address \(\WA(s)\) for any \(s\) exists in the block referenced by \((h_B, b)\).
\end{enumerate}
We say the system satisfies \emph{deposit binding} if \(\Pr[\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{bind}}^{\mathcal{B}} = 1] \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{ks}}\) for all PPT \(\mathcal{B}\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Deposit Binding]
The wormhole protocol satisfies deposit binding under knowledge soundness of \(\Pi\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By knowledge soundness, if \(\mathsf{Verify}(\pi, x) = 1\), there exists an extractor that outputs a witness \(w\) satisfying conditions (C1)--(C5) with probability \(1 - \epsilon_{\mathrm{ks}}\). Condition (C3) requires the witness to include a valid storage proof for a deposit at address \(\WA(s)\), and (C4) binds this proof to the block \((h_B, b)\). If no such deposit exists, the extractor fails, contradicting knowledge soundness. The blockchain's validation that \((h_B, b)\) refers to a real block completes the binding.
\end{proof}

\subsection{One-time withdrawal}

\begin{definition}[Double-Spend Game]
The \emph{double-spend game} \(\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{double}}^{\mathcal{B}}\) proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) creates a single deposit of amount \(v\) to address \(\WA(s)\) for adversarially chosen \(s\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) outputs two proofs \((\pi_1, x_1)\) and \((\pi_2, x_2)\) with nullifiers \(n_1 \neq n_2\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) wins if both proofs verify and both claim outputs from the same deposit.
\end{enumerate}
We say the system satisfies \emph{one-time withdrawal} if \(\Pr[\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{double}}^{\mathcal{B}} = 1] \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{ks}} + \epsilon_{\mathrm{coll}}\), where \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{coll}}\) is the collision probability of \(H\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[One-Time Withdrawal]
The wormhole protocol satisfies one-time withdrawal under knowledge soundness of \(\Pi\) and collision resistance of \(H\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Suppose \(\mathcal{B}\) wins. By knowledge soundness, there exist witnesses \(w_1, w_2\) with secrets \(s_1, s_2\) and counters \(c_1, c_2\) such that both proofs reference the same deposit. Since both reference the same deposit address, \(\WA(s_1) = \WA(s_2)\), which by collision resistance implies \(s_1 = s_2\). The counter \(c\) is stored in the on-chain deposit record and verified by the storage proof; since both proofs reference the same deposit, they must use the same counter \(c_1 = c_2\). But then \(n_1 = \Null(s_1, c_1) = \Null(s_2, c_2) = n_2\), contradicting \(n_1 \neq n_2\). Thus \(\mathcal{B}\) can only win by breaking knowledge soundness or finding a collision.
\end{proof}

Since the blockchain maintains a nullifier set and rejects duplicates, each deposit can be withdrawn at most once.

\subsection{Spend-path exclusivity}

\begin{definition}[Signature Forgery Game]
The \emph{signature forgery game} \(\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{sig}}^{\mathcal{B}}\) proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Challenger samples \(s \gets \{0,1\}^{256}\) and publishes \(\WA(s)\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) outputs a keypair \((sk, pk)\) and a signature \(\sigma\) on an arbitrary message \(m\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) wins if \(H(pk) = \WA(s)\) and \(\mathsf{SigVerify}(pk, m, \sigma) = 1\).
\end{enumerate}
We say the system satisfies \emph{spend-path exclusivity} if \(\Pr[\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{sig}}^{\mathcal{B}} = 1] \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{pre}}\), where \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{pre}}\) is the preimage resistance of \(H\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Spend-Path Exclusivity]
The wormhole protocol satisfies spend-path exclusivity under preimage resistance of \(H\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
To win, \(\mathcal{B}\) must find \(pk\) such that \(H(pk) = \WA(s) = H(H(\mathsf{salt_{wh}} \parallel s))\). This requires either:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item finding \(s'\) such that \(H(\mathsf{salt_{wh}} \parallel s')\) equals a public key \(\mathcal{B}\) controls, or
    \item finding \(pk\) such that \(H(pk)\) equals the target address.
\end{enumerate}
Both are preimage attacks. For post-quantum schemes where \(|pk| > |H|\) (e.g., ML-DSA), case (1) is impossible since a hash output cannot equal a larger public key. In either case, success probability is bounded by \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{pre}}\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Value conservation}

\begin{definition}[Inflation Game]
The \emph{inflation game} \(\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{infl}}^{\mathcal{B}}\) proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) creates deposits totaling value \(V\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) submits a sequence of valid proofs resulting in withdrawals totaling value \(W\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) wins if \(W > V \cdot (1 - f)\), where \(f\) is the fee rate.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Value Conservation]
The wormhole protocol satisfies value conservation: no adversary can win \(\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{infl}}\) except with negligible probability.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Value conservation follows from three properties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Each leaf proof enforces (C5): outputs satisfy \((v_1 + v_2) \cdot 10000 \leq v_{\mathrm{in}} \cdot (10000 - f)\).
    \item Aggregation preserves output sums; the wrapper only groups outputs to identical destinations.
    \item One-time withdrawal (Theorem 2) prevents double-counting any deposit.
\end{itemize}
Together with the blockchain's nullifier check, total settled value never exceeds total deposited value minus fees.
\end{proof}


\subsection{Anonymity sets}
\label{sec:layer0privacy}

An adversary observing a first-layer aggregate sees \(N\) nullifiers (some real, some dummies) and grouped exits. We formalize privacy through two properties: nullifier indistinguishability and transaction unlinkability.

\begin{definition}[Nullifier Indistinguishability Game]
The \emph{nullifier indistinguishability game} \(\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{null}}^{\mathcal{B}}\) proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Challenger samples secret \(s \gets \{0,1\}^{256}\), counter \(c\), and random \(r \gets \{0,1\}^{256}\).
    \item Challenger computes \(n_0 = \Null(s, c) = H(H(\mathsf{salt_{null}} \parallel s \parallel c))\) and \(n_1 = H(H(r))\).
    \item Challenger flips \(b \gets \{0,1\}\) and sends \(n_b\) to \(\mathcal{B}\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) outputs guess \(b'\).
    \item \(\mathcal{B}\) wins if \(b' = b\).
\end{enumerate}
We say nullifiers are \emph{indistinguishable} if \(|\Pr[\mathsf{Game}_{\mathrm{null}}^{\mathcal{B}} = 1] - 1/2| \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{prf}}\) for all PPT \(\mathcal{B}\), where \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{prf}}\) is the PRF advantage against \(H\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Nullifier Indistinguishability]
If \(H\) is a pseudorandom function, then real and dummy nullifiers are computationally indistinguishable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Both \(n_0 = H(H(\mathsf{salt_{null}} \parallel s \parallel c))\) and \(n_1 = H(H(r))\) are outputs of \(H\) applied to uniformly random inputs (the inner hashes). If \(\mathcal{B}\) distinguishes with advantage \(\epsilon\), we construct a PRF distinguisher with the same advantage: given oracle access to either \(H\) or a random function, query on a random input and use \(\mathcal{B}\) to distinguish. Thus \(\epsilon \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{prf}}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Anonymity set]
Fix a first-layer aggregate \(Y\) with total exit amount \(S\) and fee rate \(f\). Let \(a_1, \ldots, a_M\) be the amounts of all deposits to addresses that have never had an outgoing transaction. Since wormhole addresses cannot have outgoing transactions by construction, all wormhole deposits are included in this set. The \emph{anonymity set} \(\mathcal{A}(Y)\) is
\[
\mathcal{A}(Y) = \left\{ I \subseteq \{1,\ldots,M\} \;\middle|\; |I| \leq N \text{ and } \sum_{i \in I} a_i \geq \frac{S}{1 - f} \right\}.
\]
Computing \(|\mathcal{A}(Y)|\) is an instance of \textsc{\#SubsetSum}: given \(M\) integers, count subsets whose sum meets a target.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Transaction Unlinkability]
Let \(Y\) be a first-layer aggregate with anonymity set \(\mathcal{A}(Y)\) where \(|\mathcal{A}(Y)| = k \geq 1\). No PPT adversary can identify the true deposit subset with probability greater than \(1/k + \epsilon_{\mathrm{prf}}\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
An adversary attempting to identify the funding deposits must first determine which of the \(N\) nullifiers correspond to real deposits. By Theorem 5 (nullifier indistinguishability), the adversary cannot distinguish real from dummy nullifiers except with advantage \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{prf}}\). Conditioned on not distinguishing nullifiers, the adversary sees only the total exit amount \(S\) and must guess among all subsets in \(\mathcal{A}(Y)\) that could produce this sum. With \(k\) equally plausible subsets, random guessing succeeds with probability \(1/k\). Any strategy exceeding this probability implies either (1) distinguishing nullifiers, contradicting Theorem 5, or (2) information-theoretic leakage beyond what is revealed, which is impossible since the proof is zero-knowledge. Thus the adversary's success probability is at most \(1/k + \epsilon_{\mathrm{prf}}\).
\end{proof}

Because the proof system enforces an inequality rather than exact equality (condition C5), the adversary must consider any subset of deposits whose sum is \emph{at least} the required threshold \(T = S/(1-f)\). This is a feature: by withdrawing less than their full balance, users can select output amounts that match more potential deposit combinations, enlarging the anonymity set at the cost of burned value.

\paragraph{Complexity of \textsc{\#SubsetSum}:}
The decision version of subset sum (does any subset sum to exactly \(T\)?) is NP-complete. The counting version \textsc{\#SubsetSum} is \#P-complete \citep{subsetsum}, and no polynomial-time algorithm is known.

Our setting requires counting subsets of size at most \(N\) summing to at least \(T\). The size of \(|\mathcal{A}(Y)|\) depends on the distribution of deposit amounts relative to \(T\); when many deposits have amounts in the range of typical withdrawals, the anonymity set grows combinatorially.

\paragraph{Burn Cost and Bounded Rationality:}
A rational adversary may assume users are unwilling to burn significant value for privacy. Under bounded rationality, the effective anonymity set shrinks to subsets summing to at most \(T \cdot (1 + \delta)\) for some small \(\delta\) (e.g., 1\%). Even with this constraint, the anonymity set remains combinatorially large when deposit amounts cluster near typical withdrawal sizes. The protocol does not enforce any upper bound; users may choose their privacy-value tradeoff freely.

\subsection{Privacy leakage}

Output amounts, exit addresses, nullifiers, and timing are public. First-layer padding enlarges the anonymity set, but this is plausible deniability, not full shielding. Amount patterns, address reuse, and timing correlation remain potential deanonymization vectors.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Methodology}

Benchmarks were executed on an Apple M2 Max with 12 CPU cores and 32~GB of RAM. Circuit artifacts were pre-generated; reported times measure only proving and verification. Prove times include witness assignment and proof generation. Verify times measure verification of a pre-generated proof.

All first-layer measurements use repeated identical dummy leaf proofs with the same \texttt{block\_hash}. Second-layer benchmarks use repeated first-layer proofs.

Individual leaf proof generation takes approximately 20~ms, with verification in 1.6~ms. These times are dominated by proving; witness assignment is negligible.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Wormholes and Privacy Pools:}
EIP-7503 proposes proof-of-burn reminting via secret-derived addresses \citep{eip7503}. Privacy Pools adds compliance-aware privacy sets \citep{privacypools}. Quantus implements recursive proofs over authenticated chain state.

\paragraph{Shielded Systems:}
Zcash provides strong privacy through shielded notes; Tornado Cash popularized mixer-style privacy on Ethereum \citep{zcash,tornadocash}. Quantus offers lighter-weight private settlement anchored in native chain state.

\paragraph{Recursive Proofs:}
Halo and Plonky2 are standard recursive proof systems \citep{halo,plonky2}. Plonky3 provides Poseidon2 primitives with a separate recursion track \citep{plonky3}. We use Plonky2 with Poseidon2 for recursive composition.

\paragraph{Hash-Based Authorization:}
The Ziggy signature scheme \citep{ziggy} uses a ZK-STARK to prove knowledge of a hash preimage, replacing lattice-based signatures with hash-based proofs.

\paragraph{Post-Quantum Blockchains:}
Adoption of post-quantum signatures like ML-DSA and SLH-DSA raises questions about transaction size and verification cost \citep{mldsa}. Quantus compresses authorization into ZK proofs rather than publishing large signatures on-chain.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Wormhole addresses let a post-quantum blockchain move authorization off-chain while preserving authenticated settlement. Users deposit to secret-derived addresses, prove the deposit exists and hasn't been spent, and reveal only settlement outputs. Recursive aggregation amortizes verification. We formalized the leaf relation, analyzed first-layer privacy, proved security properties, and reported benchmark timings.
