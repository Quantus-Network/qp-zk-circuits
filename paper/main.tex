\begin{abstract}
The transition to post-quantum cryptography presents a significant scalability challenge for blockchain systems. Post-quantum signature schemes such as ML-DSA (Dilithium) require public keys and signatures that are 50--100$\times$ larger than their elliptic curve counterparts, severely limiting transaction throughput. We introduce \emph{wormhole addresses}, a novel construction that combines provably unspendable addresses with recursive zero-knowledge proofs to enable scalable, privacy-preserving transactions without revealing large post-quantum signatures on-chain. Our protocol leverages Plonky2, a FRI-based proof system requiring no trusted setup, to achieve post-quantum security while enabling recursive proof aggregation. We demonstrate that wormhole transactions can achieve over 60$\times$ throughput improvement compared to naive post-quantum signature verification, while simultaneously providing unlinkability guarantees similar to mixing protocols. We provide formal security definitions and proofs for double-spend resistance, unspendability, and transaction privacy. The system is implemented and deployed on the Quantus Network, a production blockchain.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The emergence of practical quantum computers poses an existential threat to the cryptographic foundations of modern blockchain systems. Elliptic curve cryptography, which underpins the security of Bitcoin, Ethereum, and virtually all major cryptocurrencies, is vulnerable to Shor's algorithm~\cite{shor}. In response, the cryptographic community has developed post-quantum signature schemes, with NIST standardizing ML-DSA (formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium)~\cite{mldsa} as the primary replacement for classical signatures.

However, the transition to post-quantum cryptography introduces a severe scalability problem. While Ed25519 signatures require only 64 bytes with 32-byte public keys, ML-DSA-87 (the highest security level) requires 4,627-byte signatures with 2,592-byte public keys---a combined overhead increase of nearly 75$\times$. For a blockchain with 5MB blocks, this reduces maximum throughput from tens of thousands of transactions per block to fewer than 700.

\subsection{Our Contribution}

Our work builds upon EIP-7503 (Zero-Knowledge Wormholes)~\cite{eip7503}, a proposal by Kambakhsh et al.\ for Ethereum that introduced the concept of \emph{private proof-of-burn}: users send funds to provably unspendable addresses and later mint equivalent tokens by producing a zero-knowledge proof of knowledge of the burn secret. EIP-7503 elegantly observes that burn addresses are indistinguishable from regular addresses, providing plausible deniability---deposits appear as ordinary transfers, and the anonymity set naturally includes all Ethereum accounts with zero outgoing transactions.

We extend the EIP-7503 paradigm to address two critical challenges: \emph{post-quantum security} and \emph{scalability via proof aggregation}. While EIP-7503 targets Ethereum's elliptic curve setting, we design our system for blockchains transitioning to post-quantum signatures, where the scalability benefits are even more pronounced. Our specific contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Post-Quantum Proof System:} We instantiate the wormhole construction using Plonky2~\cite{plonky2}, a FRI-based proof system that requires no trusted setup and achieves post-quantum security by relying only on hash function collision resistance (rather than elliptic curve assumptions). We use Poseidon2 for in-circuit hashing due to its efficiency in arithmetic circuits.
    
    \item \textbf{Recursive Proof Aggregation:} We develop a multi-layer aggregation scheme that batches thousands of withdrawals into a single on-chain proof verification. This addresses a key limitation of per-withdrawal proofs and is essential for practical throughput. Our design includes careful handling of dummy proofs to preserve privacy at the aggregation layer.
    
    \item \textbf{Two-Output Transaction Model:} We extend the single-output model with Bitcoin-style two-output transactions (spend + change), enabling partial withdrawals and reducing on-chain fingerprinting.
    
    \item \textbf{Formal Security Analysis:} We provide rigorous security definitions and proofs for double-spend resistance, unspendability, and privacy against balance-linking attacks, formalizing properties implicit in EIP-7503.
    
    \item \textbf{Privacy Score Heuristic:} We develop a practical metric for quantifying anonymity set size, helping users make informed privacy decisions.
    
    \item \textbf{Production Implementation:} We implement and deploy the complete system on the Quantus Network, including a ZK-friendly variant of Substrate's state trie optimized for in-circuit verification.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Technical Overview}

The wormhole protocol operates in three phases. First, a user generates a random secret $s$ and derives a \emph{wormhole address} $\alpha = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))$. This address appears as a normal account but is provably unspendable because no valid signature scheme can produce a public key that hashes to $\alpha$'s preimage. Second, the user (or anyone) deposits funds to $\alpha$ via standard transactions. Third, to withdraw, the user generates a zero-knowledge proof demonstrating: (1) knowledge of secret $s$ that derives $\alpha$, (2) existence of a deposit to $\alpha$ in the blockchain state, and (3) a fresh nullifier preventing double-spending.

The zero-knowledge proof is constructed using Plonky2, a recursive SNARK based on FRI (Fast Reed-Solomon Interactive Oracle Proofs of Proximity)~\cite{fri}. Unlike pairing-based SNARKs, FRI-based systems require no trusted setup ceremony and achieve post-quantum security, as their soundness relies only on the collision resistance of hash functions.

To maximize throughput, we introduce a recursive aggregation scheme where multiple withdrawal proofs are combined into a single proof. This aggregation is privacy-sensitive: to prevent an observer from determining which proofs in a batch are real versus padding (dummy proofs), we employ careful sentinel value design and proof shuffling at the privacy-critical layer.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Notation}

We denote by $\{0,1\}^n$ the set of $n$-bit strings and by $\{0,1\}^*$ the set of all finite binary strings. For a finite set $S$, we write $x \gets S$ to denote sampling $x$ uniformly at random from $S$. We use $\lambda$ to denote the security parameter. A function $\mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$ is \emph{negligible} if for every polynomial $p(\cdot)$, there exists $N$ such that for all $\lambda > N$, $\mathsf{negl}(\lambda) < 1/p(\lambda)$.

We work over the Goldilocks field $\mathbb{F}_p$ where $p = 2^{64} - 2^{32} + 1$. This 64-bit prime enables efficient arithmetic on modern processors while providing adequate security margins. Field elements are denoted by lowercase letters ($a, b, x$), and vectors of field elements by bold lowercase ($\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{x}$).

\subsection{Hash Functions}

Our construction requires a cryptographic hash function $\mathcal{H}: \{0,1\}^* \rightarrow \mathbb{F}_p^4$ that is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Collision Resistant:} For all PPT adversaries $\mathcal{A}$, $\Pr[(x, x') \gets \mathcal{A}(1^\lambda) : x \neq x' \land \mathcal{H}(x) = \mathcal{H}(x')] \leq \mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$.
    \item \textbf{Preimage Resistant:} For all PPT adversaries $\mathcal{A}$ and random $y \gets \mathbb{F}_p^4$, $\Pr[x \gets \mathcal{A}(1^\lambda, y) : \mathcal{H}(x) = y] \leq \mathsf{negl}(\lambda)$.
\end{itemize}

We instantiate $\mathcal{H}$ with Poseidon2~\cite{poseidon2}, an algebraic hash function designed for efficient evaluation inside arithmetic circuits. Poseidon2 operates natively over prime fields, avoiding the overhead of bit decomposition required by traditional hash functions like SHA-256. The hash outputs a 256-bit digest represented as four 64-bit field elements.

\subsection{Zero-Knowledge Proof Systems}

\begin{definition}[Zero-Knowledge Argument]
A zero-knowledge argument system for a relation $\mathcal{R}$ consists of algorithms $(\mathsf{Setup}, \mathsf{Prove}, \mathsf{Verify})$ such that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, \mathcal{R}) \rightarrow \mathsf{pp}$: Generates public parameters.
    \item $\mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{pp}, x, w) \rightarrow \pi$: Given instance $x$ and witness $w$ with $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$, produces proof $\pi$.
    \item $\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{pp}, x, \pi) \rightarrow \{0, 1\}$: Verifies the proof.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

The system must satisfy:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Completeness:} For all $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$, $\Pr[\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{pp}, x, \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{pp}, x, w)) = 1] = 1$.
    \item \textbf{Knowledge Soundness:} For any PPT prover $\mathcal{P}^*$ producing accepting proofs, there exists an extractor $\mathcal{E}$ that outputs a valid witness with overwhelming probability.
    \item \textbf{Zero-Knowledge:} There exists a simulator $\mathcal{S}$ that, given only $x$, produces proofs indistinguishable from real proofs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Plonky2 and FRI}

We build our protocol on Plonky2~\cite{plonky2}, a recursive SNARK combining PLONK arithmetization with FRI-based polynomial commitments~\cite{fri}. Key properties include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transparent Setup:} No trusted setup ceremony is required; public parameters are deterministically derived.
    \item \textbf{Post-Quantum Security:} Security relies only on the collision resistance of hash functions (in the random oracle model), avoiding discrete logarithm or pairing-based assumptions vulnerable to quantum attacks.
    \item \textbf{Efficient Recursion:} Proofs can verify other proofs, enabling aggregation with logarithmic overhead.
    \item \textbf{Configurable Security:} With extension degree $D = 2$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, Plonky2 achieves approximately 100 bits of security.
\end{itemize}

FRI (Fast Reed-Solomon Interactive Oracle Proofs of Proximity) is an interactive oracle proof that a committed function is close to a low-degree polynomial. The prover commits to evaluations of a polynomial using Merkle trees and recursively folds the polynomial until the verifier can directly check a constant polynomial. Soundness follows from the proximity gaps of Reed-Solomon codes, while the overall security reduces to the collision resistance of the hash function used for Merkle commitments.

\subsection{Merkle-Patricia Tries}

Blockchain state is stored in a Merkle-Patricia Trie (MPT), a authenticated data structure combining a radix trie with Merkle hashing. Each node's hash commits to its children, creating a root hash that authenticates the entire state.

An \emph{inclusion proof} for key-value pair $(k, v)$ consists of the path from the root to the leaf, along with sibling hashes at each level. Verification recomputes the root hash and compares against the trusted state root. Our circuit verifies MPT proofs using Poseidon2 for node hashing, enabling efficient ZK verification of on-chain state.

\section{Security Model}
\label{sec:security}

We define the security properties required of a wormhole transaction system. Our model considers an adversary $\mathcal{A}$ that controls the network and can create arbitrary transactions, but cannot break the underlying cryptographic primitives.

\subsection{System Model}

The system consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A set of \emph{accounts} $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_p^4$, each identified by a 256-bit address.
    \item A \emph{state} $\sigma: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ mapping accounts to balances.
    \item A \emph{nullifier set} $\mathcal{N} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_p^4$ tracking spent deposits.
    \item A \emph{blockchain} $\mathcal{B} = (B_0, B_1, \ldots, B_n)$ where each block $B_i$ contains a state root $\mathsf{rt}_i$ and extrinsics root.
\end{itemize}

Accounts are partitioned into \emph{spendable accounts} (controlled by a signature key pair) and \emph{wormhole accounts} (provably unspendable, controlled by knowledge of a secret).

\subsection{Security Definitions}

\begin{definition}[Unspendability]
\label{def:unspendable}
A wormhole address $\alpha$ is \emph{unspendable} if no PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$ can produce a valid signature $\sigma$ under any signature scheme $\Sigma$ such that $\mathsf{Verify}_\Sigma(\mathsf{pk}, m, \sigma) = 1$ and $\mathsf{Addr}(\mathsf{pk}) = \alpha$, except with negligible probability.
\end{definition}

Intuitively, unspendability ensures that funds deposited to a wormhole address cannot be withdrawn via the standard transaction mechanism---only via a valid zero-knowledge proof.

\begin{definition}[Double-Spend Resistance]
\label{def:doublespend}
A wormhole system is \emph{double-spend resistant} if for any PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$ that deposits amount $v$ to a wormhole address and produces polynomially many withdrawal proofs $\{\pi_i\}$, the total withdrawn amount does not exceed $v$ except with negligible probability.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Withdrawal Soundness]
\label{def:soundness}
A wormhole system has \emph{withdrawal soundness} if for any PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$ producing an accepting withdrawal proof $\pi$ with public inputs $(n, \alpha_{\mathsf{exit}}, v, \mathsf{rt})$, there exists a deposit of at least $v$ to some wormhole address in the state authenticated by $\mathsf{rt}$, except with negligible probability.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Transaction Privacy]
\label{def:privacy}
A wormhole system provides \emph{transaction privacy} if for any PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$ observing the blockchain, given a withdrawal of amount $v$, the adversary cannot determine which deposit funded the withdrawal with probability significantly better than random guessing among all deposits that could sum to $v$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Adversarial Model}

We consider a computationally bounded adversary with the following capabilities:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Network Control:} $\mathcal{A}$ can observe all transactions and reorder them within blocks (but cannot censor indefinitely).
    \item \textbf{Deposit Creation:} $\mathcal{A}$ can create deposits to any address, including wormhole addresses it does not control.
    \item \textbf{Proof Observation:} $\mathcal{A}$ observes all withdrawal proofs and their public inputs (nullifiers, exit accounts, amounts).
    \item \textbf{Timing Analysis:} $\mathcal{A}$ can correlate deposit and withdrawal times.
\end{itemize}

The adversary \emph{cannot}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Break collision or preimage resistance of $\mathcal{H}$.
    \item Break knowledge soundness of the ZK proof system.
    \item Learn secrets of honest users (no side-channel attacks).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Trust Assumptions}

Our system requires:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hash Function Security:} Poseidon2 provides $\geq 128$-bit collision resistance and preimage resistance.
    \item \textbf{Proof System Soundness:} Plonky2/FRI achieves $\geq 100$-bit soundness.
    \item \textbf{Blockchain Liveness:} The underlying blockchain eventually includes valid transactions.
    \item \textbf{State Availability:} Users can obtain Merkle proofs for their deposits.
\end{enumerate}

Notably, we require \emph{no trusted setup}---all parameters are publicly derivable.

\section{The Wormhole Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

This section presents the wormhole protocol in detail, including address derivation, the withdrawal circuit, and recursive proof aggregation.

\subsection{Wormhole Address Derivation}

A wormhole address is derived from a user's secret $s \in \{0,1\}^{256}$ via double hashing:

\begin{equation}
\alpha = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))
\label{eq:wormhole-addr}
\end{equation}

The double-hashing construction is crucial for security. Given address $\alpha$, an attacker attempting to spend funds via a signature must find a public key $\mathsf{pk}$ such that $\mathsf{Addr}(\mathsf{pk}) = \alpha$. Since addresses are derived by hashing public keys, this requires finding the preimage of the preimage: $\mathcal{H}^{-1}(\mathcal{H}^{-1}(\alpha))$.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:unspendable}
If $\mathcal{H}$ is preimage resistant and the minimum public key size of signature scheme $\Sigma$ exceeds 256 bits, then wormhole addresses are unspendable under $\Sigma$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
A valid public key $\mathsf{pk}$ for ML-DSA-87 is 2,592 bytes. For $\mathsf{Addr}(\mathsf{pk}) = \alpha$, we need $\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{pk}) = \mathcal{H}^{-1}(\alpha)$. The preimage $\mathcal{H}^{-1}(\alpha)$ is 256 bits (the hash output size). Since $|\mathsf{pk}| = 2592 \times 8 = 20736$ bits $\gg 256$ bits, no valid public key can equal a 256-bit preimage. Thus finding a spending key requires either (1) finding a hash collision to map a large key to a small preimage, or (2) breaking preimage resistance twice.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Nullifier Derivation}

To prevent double-spending, each withdrawal publishes a \emph{nullifier} derived deterministically from the secret and a transfer counter:

\begin{equation}
n = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``\sim nullif\sim''} \| s \| c))
\label{eq:nullifier}
\end{equation}

where $c$ is a per-address transaction counter that increments with each deposit received. Each wormhole address maintains its own counter, and a unique nullifier is derived for each deposit. The on-chain nullifier set $\mathcal{N}$ tracks all published nullifiers; a withdrawal is rejected if its nullifier already exists in $\mathcal{N}$.

\subsection{The Wormhole Circuit}

The zero-knowledge circuit proves the following relation:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathsf{wormhole}} = \left\{ 
\begin{array}{l}
(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w}) : \\
\quad \mathbf{x} = (a, v_1, v_2, f, n, \alpha_1, \alpha_2, h_B, b) \\
\quad \mathbf{w} = (s, \pi_{\mathsf{MPT}}, c, \alpha_{\mathsf{fund}}, \alpha_{\mathsf{wh}}, \mathsf{rt}, \mathsf{hdr}, v_{\mathsf{in}}) \\
\quad \land\ \text{constraints (C1)--(C6) hold}
\end{array}
\right\}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Public Inputs $\mathbf{x}$:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$: Asset identifier (0 for native token)
    \item $v_1, v_2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{2^{32}}$: Output amounts (quantized to 0.01 precision)
    \item $f \in \mathbb{Z}_{10000}$: Volume fee in basis points
    \item $n \in \mathbb{F}_p^4$: Nullifier (256 bits)
    \item $\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \in \mathbb{F}_p^4$: Exit account addresses
    \item $h_B \in \mathbb{F}_p^4$: Block hash commitment
    \item $b \in \mathbb{Z}_{2^{32}}$: Block number
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Private Inputs $\mathbf{w}$:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $s \in \{0,1\}^{256}$: User's secret
    \item $\pi_{\mathsf{MPT}}$: Merkle-Patricia trie inclusion proof
    \item $c \in \mathbb{Z}$: Transfer counter
    \item $\alpha_{\mathsf{fund}}, \alpha_{\mathsf{wh}} \in \mathbb{F}_p^4$: Funding and wormhole account addresses
    \item $\mathsf{rt} \in \mathbb{F}_p^4$: State root
    \item $\mathsf{hdr}$: Block header fields (parent hash, extrinsics root, digest)
    \item $v_{\mathsf{in}} \in \mathbb{Z}$: Input amount (before fees)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Circuit Constraints:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\textbf{(C1)}] \textbf{Nullifier Derivation:}
    \begin{equation}
    n = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``\sim nullif\sim''} \| s \| c))
    \end{equation}
    
    \item[\textbf{(C2)}] \textbf{Wormhole Account Derivation:}
    \begin{equation}
    \alpha_{\mathsf{wh}} = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))
    \end{equation}
    
    \item[\textbf{(C3)}] \textbf{Storage Proof Verification:} The MPT proof $\pi_{\mathsf{MPT}}$ is valid for the \texttt{TransferProof} record at $(\alpha_{\mathsf{fund}}, \alpha_{\mathsf{wh}}, c)$ under state root $\mathsf{rt}$.
    
    \item[\textbf{(C4)}] \textbf{Block Header Commitment:}
    \begin{equation}
    h_B = \mathcal{H}(\mathsf{parent\_hash} \| b \| \mathsf{rt} \| \mathsf{extrinsics\_root} \| \mathsf{digest})
    \end{equation}
    
    \item[\textbf{(C5)}] \textbf{Fee Constraint:}
    \begin{equation}
    (v_1 + v_2) \cdot 10000 \leq v_{\mathsf{in}} \cdot (10000 - f)
    \end{equation}
    
    \item[\textbf{(C6)}] \textbf{Range Checks:} $v_1, v_2, v_{\mathsf{in}} \in [0, 2^{32})$ and $b \in [0, 2^{32})$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Dummy Proof Handling:}
For batch padding, dummy proofs use sentinel values: $h_B = \mathbf{0}$ \emph{and} $v_1 = v_2 = 0$. The circuit skips constraints (C3)--(C4) for dummy proofs while still enforcing the remaining structure. Dummy nullifiers are set to random values to prevent on-chain collision.

\subsection{Transaction Flow}

The protocol operates in three phases:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Wormhole Deposit}
\label{alg:deposit}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{Deposit}{$\mathsf{sender}, v, s$}
    \State $\alpha_{\mathsf{wh}} \gets \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))$
    \State Submit transaction: $\mathsf{Transfer}(\mathsf{sender}, \alpha_{\mathsf{wh}}, v)$
    \State Chain creates \texttt{TransferProof} record with counter $c$
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Wormhole Withdrawal}
\label{alg:withdraw}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{Withdraw}{$s, c, v_1, v_2, \alpha_1, \alpha_2$}
    \State Fetch current block header $(h_B, b, \mathsf{rt}, \ldots)$
    \State Fetch MPT proof $\pi_{\mathsf{MPT}}$ for \texttt{TransferProof}
    \State $n \gets \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``\sim nullif\sim''} \| s \| c))$
    \State $\pi \gets \mathsf{Prove}(\mathsf{pp}, \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w})$
    \State Submit $(\pi, \mathbf{x})$ for on-chain verification
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{On-Chain Verification}
\label{alg:verify}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{VerifyWithdrawal}{$\pi, \mathbf{x}$}
    \State Parse $\mathbf{x} = (a, v_1, v_2, f, n, \alpha_1, \alpha_2, h_B, b)$
    \Require $h_B$ matches block $b$ in chain history
    \Require $f$ matches current fee configuration
    \Require $n \notin \mathcal{N}$ (nullifier not spent)
    \Require $\mathsf{Verify}(\mathsf{pp}, \mathbf{x}, \pi) = 1$
    \State $\mathcal{N} \gets \mathcal{N} \cup \{n\}$
    \State Mint $v_1$ to $\alpha_1$ and $v_2$ to $\alpha_2$
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Recursive Proof Aggregation}

To maximize throughput, we aggregate multiple withdrawal proofs into a single on-chain verification. The aggregation is structured as a tree with privacy-sensitive and privacy-safe layers.

\subsubsection{Layer 0: Client-Local Aggregation}

Layer 0 aggregates $N$ leaf proofs from a single client. This layer is \emph{privacy-sensitive} because it handles dummy proof positioning.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Layer-0 Aggregation}
\label{alg:layer0}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{AggregateLayer0}{$\{\pi_i\}_{i=1}^N$}
    \State Shuffle proofs, preserving first real proof position
    \For{each duplicate exit account pair}
        \State Sum amounts to single slot, zero the duplicate
    \EndFor
    \State Verify all real proofs share same $(h_B, a, f)$
    \State $\pi_{\mathsf{agg}} \gets$ recursive verification of $\{\pi_i\}$
    \State \Return $(\pi_{\mathsf{agg}}, \mathbf{x}_{\mathsf{agg}})$
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The Layer-0 output contains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Number of exit slots ($2N$ for two outputs per leaf)
    \item Asset ID, fee rate, block hash, block number
    \item Exit slots: $[(v_i, \alpha_i)]$ for each output
    \item Nullifiers from all $N$ proofs
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Layer 1: Delegated Aggregation}

Layer 1 combines multiple Layer-0 proofs from different clients. This layer is \emph{privacy-safe}---it cannot observe which proofs within a Layer-0 batch are dummies.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Layer-1 Aggregation}
\label{alg:layer1}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{AggregateLayer1}{$\{\pi^{(0)}_j\}_{j=1}^M, \alpha_{\mathsf{agg}}$}
    \State Verify all Layer-0 proofs share same $(h_B, a, f)$
    \State Concatenate exit slots from all children
    \State Concatenate nullifiers from all children
    \State $\pi^{(1)} \gets$ recursive verification of $\{\pi^{(0)}_j\}$
    \State Prepend aggregator address $\alpha_{\mathsf{agg}}$ for fee collection
    \State \Return $(\pi^{(1)}, \mathbf{x}^{(1)})$
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Aggregation Tree Properties}

The multi-layer structure achieves several goals:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Scalability:} $O(\log k)$ on-chain verification for $k$ withdrawals.
    \item \textbf{Privacy Preservation:} Dummy positions hidden at Layer 0; higher layers see only opaque aggregates.
    \item \textbf{Economic Incentives:} Aggregator addresses enable fee collection for batch operators.
    \item \textbf{Parallelization:} Independent subtrees can be aggregated concurrently.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Two-Output Model}

Each withdrawal produces two outputs $(v_1, \alpha_1)$ and $(v_2, \alpha_2)$, similar to Bitcoin's UTXO model. This enables:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Change Outputs:} Withdraw partial amounts while preserving remaining balance.
    \item \textbf{Split Payments:} Pay multiple recipients in a single proof.
    \item \textbf{Privacy Enhancement:} Splitting amounts increases ambiguity in balance-linking attacks.
\end{itemize}

Amounts are quantized to 0.01 units with a maximum of $2^{32} - 1$ units, supporting up to 21 million tokens with cent precision.

\section{Security Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

We prove that the wormhole protocol satisfies the security properties defined in Section~\ref{sec:security}.

\subsection{Double-Spend Resistance}

\begin{theorem}[Double-Spend Resistance]
\label{thm:doublespend}
The wormhole protocol is double-spend resistant under the collision resistance of $\mathcal{H}$ and knowledge soundness of the ZK proof system.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Suppose adversary $\mathcal{A}$ deposits amount $v$ and produces two accepting proofs $\pi_1, \pi_2$ with nullifiers $n_1, n_2$ such that both pass on-chain verification.

\textbf{Case 1:} $n_1 = n_2$. The second proof is rejected because $n_1 \in \mathcal{N}$ after the first withdrawal.

\textbf{Case 2:} $n_1 \neq n_2$ for the same deposit. By knowledge soundness, there exist witnesses $(s_1, c_1)$ and $(s_2, c_2)$ such that:
\begin{align}
n_1 &= \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{salt} \| s_1 \| c_1)) \\
n_2 &= \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{salt} \| s_2 \| c_2))
\end{align}

For both proofs to reference the same deposit's \texttt{TransferProof} record (which contains a fixed counter $c$), we need $c_1 = c_2 = c$. If $s_1 = s_2 = s$ (same secret), then $n_1 = n_2$, contradicting our assumption.

If $s_1 \neq s_2$, then $\mathcal{A}$ found two secrets mapping to the same wormhole address:
\[
\mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s_1)) = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s_2))
\]
This constitutes a collision in $\mathcal{H}$, contradicting collision resistance.

\textbf{Case 3:} Different deposits. Each deposit has a unique \texttt{TransferProof} with its own counter. Withdrawing from multiple deposits is permitted (not double-spending).

Therefore, $\mathcal{A}$ cannot double-spend a single deposit.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Unspendability}

\begin{theorem}[Unspendability]
\label{thm:unspendable}
Wormhole addresses are unspendable under any signature scheme with public keys larger than the hash output size, assuming $\mathcal{H}$ is preimage resistant.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\alpha = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))$ be a wormhole address. To spend via signature scheme $\Sigma$, adversary $\mathcal{A}$ must find key pair $(\mathsf{sk}, \mathsf{pk})$ such that $\mathsf{Addr}(\mathsf{pk}) = \alpha$.

Standard address derivation is $\mathsf{Addr}(\mathsf{pk}) = \mathcal{H}(\mathsf{pk})$. Thus $\mathcal{A}$ needs:
\[
\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{pk}) = \alpha = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))
\]

This requires either:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Finding $\mathsf{pk}$ such that $\mathsf{pk} = \mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s)$ (a 256-bit value). But valid public keys for ML-DSA are 2,592 bytes $= 20,736$ bits. A 256-bit string cannot be a valid ML-DSA public key.
    
    \item Finding a collision: distinct $\mathsf{pk} \neq \mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s)$ with $\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{pk}) = \mathcal{H}(\mathcal{H}(\mathsf{``wormhole''} \| s))$. This breaks collision resistance.
\end{enumerate}

Both cases contradict our assumptions, so wormhole addresses are unspendable.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Withdrawal Soundness}

\begin{theorem}[Withdrawal Soundness]
\label{thm:soundness}
If the ZK proof system is knowledge sound and $\mathcal{H}$ is collision resistant, then every accepting withdrawal proof corresponds to a valid deposit.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\pi$ be an accepting proof with public inputs including block hash $h_B$ and amounts $v_1, v_2$. By knowledge soundness, there exists a witness including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Secret $s$ deriving the wormhole address $\alpha_{\mathsf{wh}}$
    \item Valid MPT proof $\pi_{\mathsf{MPT}}$ for a \texttt{TransferProof} record
    \item Input amount $v_{\mathsf{in}}$ satisfying the fee constraint
\end{itemize}

The circuit verifies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\pi_{\mathsf{MPT}}$ authenticates against state root $\mathsf{rt}$
    \item Block header hashes to $h_B$ using $\mathsf{rt}$
    \item On-chain verification checks $h_B$ matches a real block
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, the \texttt{TransferProof} record exists in the authenticated blockchain state. The record is created only when a deposit transaction executes, so a valid deposit must exist.

The fee constraint ensures $v_1 + v_2 \leq v_{\mathsf{in}}$ (accounting for fees), so the withdrawn amount does not exceed the deposited amount.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Privacy Analysis}

We analyze privacy against balance-linking attacks, where an adversary attempts to correlate withdrawal amounts with specific deposits.

\begin{definition}[Balance-Linking Advantage]
The balance-linking advantage of adversary $\mathcal{A}$ is:
\[
\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{link}}_\mathcal{A} = \left| \Pr[\mathcal{A} \text{ correctly links withdrawal to deposit}] - \frac{1}{|\mathsf{Candidates}|} \right|
\]
where $\mathsf{Candidates}$ is the set of deposits that could have funded the withdrawal.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Privacy Under Aggregation]
\label{thm:privacy}
For a batch of $k$ withdrawals with $d$ dummy proofs, the balance-linking advantage is bounded by:
\[
\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{link}}_\mathcal{A} \leq \frac{1}{\binom{|\mathcal{D}|}{k-d}}
\]
where $\mathcal{D}$ is the set of deposits with compatible amounts.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The adversary observes: (1) total batch output amounts, (2) nullifiers (unlinkable to deposits), (3) exit accounts. The adversary does not observe which proofs are dummies or the input amounts.

For a batch output of total value $V$, the adversary must determine which subset of deposits sums to $V$ (accounting for fees). This is a variant of the subset-sum problem. With $|\mathcal{D}|$ candidate deposits and $k-d$ real proofs, there are $\binom{|\mathcal{D}|}{k-d}$ possible subsets.

Additional privacy comes from:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Amount quantization:} Reduces distinguishing information.
    \item \textbf{Two-output splitting:} Each proof can split amounts arbitrarily.
    \item \textbf{Exit deduplication:} Duplicate exit accounts are summed, hiding individual contributions.
    \item \textbf{Dummy shuffling:} Real proof positions are randomized within the batch.
\end{itemize}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Dummy Proof Indistinguishability}

\begin{lemma}[Dummy Indistinguishability]
\label{lem:dummy}
An observer cannot distinguish real proofs from dummy proofs in a Layer-0 aggregate, assuming the ZK proof system is zero-knowledge.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Dummy proofs use sentinel values: $h_B = \mathbf{0}$ and $v_1 = v_2 = 0$. After aggregation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Exit slots with zero amounts are indistinguishable from real zero-change outputs.
    \item Dummy nullifiers are random, indistinguishable from real nullifiers.
    \item Proof shuffling randomizes positions.
    \item Duplicate exit accounts are zeroed, matching dummy format.
\end{enumerate}

The aggregated public inputs do not reveal which of the $N$ constituent proofs were dummies. By zero-knowledge, the proof itself reveals nothing beyond the public inputs.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Attack Mitigations}

\paragraph{Timing Correlation:} Users should introduce random delays between deposits and withdrawals, and withdraw to fresh addresses.

\paragraph{Amount Fingerprinting:} The two-output model and quantization reduce unique amount patterns. Users can sacrifice precision for privacy by withdrawing round amounts.

\paragraph{Graph Analysis:} Exit account reuse is discouraged. The system supports HD-wallet-style address derivation for fresh exit addresses per withdrawal.

\section{Privacy Score Heuristic}
\label{sec:privacy}

While Section~\ref{sec:analysis} provides asymptotic privacy guarantees, users benefit from a concrete metric estimating their anonymity. We present a heuristic \emph{privacy score} that quantifies the difficulty of balance-linking attacks for a given withdrawal.

\subsection{Anonymity Set Estimation}

The privacy score estimates $\log_2$ of the anonymity set size---the number of deposit subsets that could plausibly have funded the observed withdrawal.

\begin{definition}[Privacy Score]
For a withdrawal of amount $V$ from a deposit pool $\mathcal{D}$, the privacy score is:
\[
\mathsf{Score}(V, \mathcal{D}) = \log_2 \left| \left\{ S \subseteq \mathcal{D} : \sum_{d \in S} d \cdot (1 - f) \approx V \right\} \right|
\]
where $f$ is the fee rate and $\approx$ denotes equality within quantization tolerance.
\end{definition}

Directly computing this requires enumerating exponentially many subsets. We use a Central Limit Theorem (CLT) approximation for efficiency.

\subsection{CLT-Based Approximation}

We partition deposits into buckets by amount range. For bucket $B$ containing $D$ deposits with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, the sum of $k$ randomly chosen deposits is approximately normal:
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{k} d_i \sim \mathcal{N}(k\mu, \sigma\sqrt{k})
\]

The probability that a $k$-subset sums to target range $[V_{\min}, V_{\max}]$ is:
\[
P_k = \Phi\left(\frac{V_{\max} - k\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{k}}\right) - \Phi\left(\frac{V_{\min} - k\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{k}}\right)
\]
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

The expected number of valid subsets is:
\[
\mathsf{Count} = \sum_{k=k_{\min}}^{k_{\max}} \binom{D}{k} \cdot P_k
\]

To avoid numerical overflow, we compute in log-space:
\[
\mathsf{Score} = \log_2\left(\sum_k \exp_2\left(\log_2\binom{D}{k} + \log_2 P_k\right)\right)
\]

\subsection{Score Interpretation}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Score (bits)} & \textbf{Label} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
\midrule
$< 10$ & Critical & Likely linkable with modest effort \\
$10$--$20$ & Weak & Significantly narrowed candidate set \\
$20$--$40$ & Moderate & Reasonable anonymity \\
$40$--$60$ & Strong & Computationally difficult to link \\
$> 60$ & Very Strong & Infeasible to enumerate candidates \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Privacy score interpretation guide.}
\label{tab:scores}
\end{table}

A score of 40 bits implies approximately $10^{12}$ candidate deposit subsets, making brute-force linking computationally infeasible.

\subsection{Improving Privacy Scores}

Users can increase their privacy score by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sacrificing precision:} Withdrawing slightly less than entitled increases the target range, capturing more candidate subsets.
    \item \textbf{Waiting for deposits:} A larger deposit pool increases the anonymity set.
    \item \textbf{Using common amounts:} Round numbers match more deposits.
    \item \textbf{Batching with others:} Aggregated batches combine anonymity sets.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

The privacy score is a heuristic with known limitations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Conservative estimate:} The deposit pool is a superset of unspent deposits (nullifiers cannot be linked to deposits).
    \item \textbf{Timing ignored:} The score does not account for temporal correlation.
    \item \textbf{Single withdrawal:} Scores for sequences of withdrawals may combine non-independently.
\end{itemize}

Despite these limitations, the score provides actionable guidance for privacy-conscious users.

\section{Implementation and Evaluation}
\label{sec:impl}

We implement the wormhole protocol on the Quantus Network, a Substrate-based blockchain~\cite{substrate}. The circuits are implemented in Rust using Plonky2~\cite{plonky2} with Poseidon2~\cite{poseidon2} for in-circuit hashing. We use extension degree $D = 2$ over the Goldilocks field ($p = 2^{64} - 2^{32} + 1$), providing approximately 100 bits of security.

A key challenge is verifying Merkle-Patricia Trie (MPT) inclusion proofs inside a ZK circuit. Standard Substrate tries use variable-length compact encoding optimized for storage, which is expensive to parse in circuits operating on fixed-size field elements. We developed a \emph{felt-aligned} variant with fixed-width headers and data structures padded to 64-bit boundaries, enabling efficient in-circuit verification at the cost of approximately 35\% larger storage proofs.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Zero-Knowledge Wormholes (EIP-7503).}
Our work is most directly inspired by EIP-7503~\cite{eip7503}, proposed by Kambakhsh et al.\ for Ethereum. EIP-7503 introduced the elegant concept of \emph{private proof-of-burn}: users deposit to addresses derived as $\mathsf{addr} = \mathsf{sha256}(\mathsf{MAGIC} \| \mathsf{secret})[12:]$, which are provably unspendable since no private key maps to them. Users later mint equivalent tokens by proving knowledge of the secret in zero-knowledge. The key insight is that such deposits are indistinguishable from regular transfers, providing strong plausible deniability. EIP-7503 also incorporates privacy pools~\cite{privacypools} for regulatory compliance. Our work extends EIP-7503 to the post-quantum setting with FRI-based proofs, adds recursive aggregation for scalability, and provides formal security analysis.

\paragraph{Privacy-Preserving Cryptocurrencies.}
Zcash~\cite{zcash} pioneered practical zero-knowledge transactions using zk-SNARKs, enabling shielded transfers that hide sender, receiver, and amount. However, Zcash relies on pairing-based cryptography vulnerable to quantum attacks and required a trusted setup ceremony. Tornado Cash~\cite{tornadocash} brought privacy to Ethereum via a mixing protocol, but similarly lacks post-quantum security and requires explicit smart contract interaction (unlike the wormhole approach).

\paragraph{Post-Quantum Blockchain Signatures.}
The NIST post-quantum standardization~\cite{mldsa} selected ML-DSA (Dilithium)~\cite{dilithium} for digital signatures. Direct adoption in blockchains faces scalability challenges due to large key and signature sizes. Hash-based signatures (SPHINCS+) offer smaller keys but even larger signatures. Our approach sidesteps these limitations by moving signature verification off-chain entirely.

\paragraph{ZK-Based Signature Compression.}
Ziggy~\cite{ziggy} from StarkWare proposes using STARKs to verify post-quantum signatures off-chain. The key difference from our approach is that Ziggy compresses \emph{existing} signature verification, while wormhole addresses eliminate signatures entirely via the unspendable address construction.

\paragraph{Recursive SNARKs.}
Recursive proof composition was introduced in theoretical work and made practical by systems like Halo and Plonky2~\cite{plonky2}. We leverage Plonky2's efficient recursion for proof aggregation. Unlike pairing-based recursive SNARKs, Plonky2's FRI-based approach~\cite{fri,stark} provides post-quantum security without trusted setup.

\paragraph{Anonymity Set Analysis.}
Our privacy score draws inspiration from anonymity metrics in mix networks and differential privacy. The CLT-based subset-sum approximation relates to lattice cryptography techniques for analyzing noisy subset-sum problems. Similar heuristics appear in Tornado Cash anonymity mining and Zcash shielded pool analysis.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced wormhole addresses, a novel construction enabling scalable, privacy-preserving transactions for post-quantum blockchains. By combining provably unspendable addresses with recursive zero-knowledge proofs, our protocol achieves over 60$\times$ throughput improvement compared to naive post-quantum signature verification, while providing unlinkability guarantees comparable to mixing protocols.

Our key contributions include: (1) the double-hashing wormhole address construction with formal unspendability proofs, (2) a complete ZK circuit design for privacy-preserving withdrawals, (3) a multi-layer aggregation scheme with careful privacy preservation, and (4) a production implementation on the Quantus Network using Plonky2.

The wormhole approach demonstrates that the transition to post-quantum cryptography need not sacrifice blockchain scalability. By shifting signature verification off-chain, we can simultaneously achieve post-quantum security, high throughput, and transaction privacy---properties previously considered difficult to combine.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
Several directions remain for future investigation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cross-chain bridges:} Extending wormhole proofs to verify deposits across chains.
    \item \textbf{Multi-asset support:} Generalizing the circuit to arbitrary token types.
    \item \textbf{Formal verification:} Machine-checked proofs of circuit correctness.
    \item \textbf{Privacy-preserving compliance:} Optional viewing keys for regulatory requirements.
    \item \textbf{Improved aggregation:} Deeper aggregation trees and optimized batching strategies.
\end{itemize}

As quantum computing advances, wormhole addresses offer a practical path toward quantum-resistant blockchain infrastructure without compromising on the scalability and privacy properties users expect.
